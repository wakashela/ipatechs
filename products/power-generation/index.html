Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between prime and standby generator ratings?

Standby rating (ESP — Emergency Standby Power) is for generators that run only during utility outages, typically for a few hours per month maximum. The generator can supply 100% of its nameplate rating with a 10% overload for 1 hour in 12, but average load must not exceed 70% of rating over 24 hours. Prime rating (PRP — Prime Power) is for generators that run as the primary power source for unlimited hours, typically at variable load with an average load factor of 70% over 24 hours. Continuous rating (COP) is for 100% load, unlimited hours — typically applied to baseload power plants. Selecting the wrong rating leads to premature engine wear, excessive fuel consumption, and warranty invalidation. Our engineers calculate your load profile to determine the correct rating category.

How critical is voltage regulation for my application?

Voltage regulation requirements depend on your connected load. Standard AVR regulation of ±1% is adequate for general industrial loads — motors, pumps, conveyors. However, sensitive electronics (data centres, telecom equipment, medical imaging, CNC machines) require PMG-excited alternators that achieve ±0.5% or better regulation and maintain voltage during severe non-linear load conditions. PMG excitation also provides sustained short-circuit current for 10–15 seconds, essential for coordinated tripping of downstream circuit breakers. Our engineers assess your load sensitivity and specify the correct excitation system — AREP or PMG — for your application.

What maintenance schedule should I follow for my diesel generator?

A proper maintenance schedule is the single biggest factor in generator reliability. We recommend: Daily/Weekly — visual inspection of fuel level, coolant level, oil level, battery charger status, control panel indicators, and exercise run (no-load for 10 minutes) for standby sets. Every 250 hours or 6 months — oil and filter change, fuel filter change, air filter inspection, belt tension check, coolant concentration test, and battery terminal cleaning. Every 1,000 hours or 12 months — full engine service (valve clearance, injector test, coolant replacement), alternator insulation resistance test, AVR calibration check, protection relay functional test, and load-bank testing at full rated load for minimum 2 hours. Every 10,000 hours — major overhaul including cylinder head, pistons, liners, bearings, and turbocharger inspection. We offer service contracts tailored to your operating hours and site conditions.

How do I calculate the correct generator size for my facility?

Generator sizing involves four key factors: (1) Total running load in kW — sum of all connected equipment rated power, applying a diversity factor if not all loads run simultaneously. (2) Starting kVA requirements — large motors draw 3–6 times their running current during starting. The alternator must supply this surge without excessive voltage dip. (3) Load step capability — large loads switching on/off must not cause frequency or voltage excursions beyond acceptable limits. (4) Future expansion — size for projected load growth over 5-10 years. As a rule of thumb, motor starting load often dictates generator size rather than total running kW. Our engineers perform detailed load analysis including locked-rotor kVA, starting sequence, and non-linear load harmonic assessment.
