Frequently Asked Questions

VFD vs Soft Starter — Which One Should I Choose?

The choice depends on your application's need for speed control. A soft starter reduces inrush current and provides smooth ramp-up/down, making it ideal for fixed-speed applications like pumps, fans, and compressors where you only need to manage starting torque. It is simpler, cheaper, and generates no harmonics. A variable frequency drive (VFD) gives you full speed control throughout operation, enabling energy savings of 20-60% on centrifugal loads by running the motor at the exact speed required. Use a VFD when your process demands variable flow, pressure, or speed — and when energy savings justify the higher upfront cost. Our engineers can run a payback calculation for your specific application.

What Is the Typical ATS Changeover Time?

Standard automatic transfer switches (ATS) using motorised circuit breakers or contactors typically achieve changeover times of 0.5 to 3 seconds from mains failure to generator supply. This includes the dead-bus transition time and generator start delay. For critical loads that cannot tolerate even a momentary interruption — such as hospital operating theatres, data centres, and continuous process control systems — we supply static transfer switches (STS) with sub-cycle (<20ms) switching. Open-transition (break-before-make) is standard; closed-transition (make-before-break) ATS is available where synchronised sources permit.

Busbar vs Cable Distribution — Which Is Better for My Building?

Busbar trunking systems (Schneider Canalis or Siemens SIVACON 8PS) offer significant advantages over cable in multi-storey commercial and industrial buildings: faster installation (up to 60% less labour), easy tap-off units for future load changes, compact footprint in vertical risers, and lower voltage drop over long runs. Cable distribution remains preferable for short distances, areas with complex routing, or where the installed cost of busbar is hard to justify. For buildings with evolving tenant loads — hotels, offices, shopping centres — busbar almost always wins the total cost of ownership calculation.

Do VFDs Create Harmonics, and How Are They Mitigated?

Yes, all VFDs generate harmonic currents because of the rectification stage that converts AC to DC. Standard 6-pulse drives without mitigation can produce total harmonic current distortion (THDi) of 30-45%, which can cause transformer overheating, conductor derating, and nuisance tripping of protective devices. Mitigation options include: built-in DC link chokes (standard on most modern Schneider ATV and Siemens SINAMICS drives, reducing THDi to ~35-40%), 12-pulse or 18-pulse rectifier configurations (THDi <10%), active front-end (AFE) drives with near-sinusoidal input current (THDi <5%), and passive or active harmonic filters retrofitted to existing installations. Our engineering team conducts harmonic studies using ETAP or SKM PowerTools to design the right mitigation strategy for your installation.
