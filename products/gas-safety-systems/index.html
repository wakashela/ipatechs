Frequently Asked Questions

What ATEX zone classification does my site need?

ATEX zones are defined by the likelihood and duration of explosive atmosphere presence. Zone 0: explosive atmosphere present continuously or for long periods (inside storage tanks, process vessels). Zone 1: explosive atmosphere likely to occur occasionally during normal operation (around vent openings, filling stations). Zone 2: explosive atmosphere not likely during normal operation, and if it occurs will only persist for a short period (areas adjacent to Zone 1, pipe flanges, valve stems). We review your site's hazardous area classification drawing (HAC) to verify the correct equipment zone, gas group (IIA, IIB, IIC), and temperature class (T1-T6) before specifying any safety device.

What SIL rating should my safety systems have?

Safety Integrity Level (SIL) is determined by your process hazard analysis (HAZOP/LOPA study). SIL 1 is typically sufficient for non-critical alarms and monitoring. SIL 2 — a common requirement in Tanzanian gas processing — applies to emergency shutdown (ESD) valves, flame and gas detection systems, and overpressure protection loops where failure could result in serious injury or major equipment damage. SIL 3 is reserved for the highest consequence scenarios — offshore blowout preventers, high-inventory toxic gas storage — and is rarely specified onshore. Honeywell Searchpoint Optima Plus and XNX detectors are certified to SIL 2. Rotork IQ3 actuators with the SIL option enable partial stroke testing as part of a SIL-rated ESD loop.

What actuator duty cycle do I need — S2 or S4?

The actuator duty classification determines how the motor manages heat during repeated starts. S2 (short-time duty) is suitable for isolation/shutdown valves that operate infrequently — open or close a few times per shift or per day. The motor cools fully between operations. S4 (intermittent duty with starting) is required for modulating control valves that make frequent small adjustments — up to 1,200 starts per hour. Rotork IQ3 actuators are available in both S2 and S4 configurations. Specifying S2 for a modulating application causes motor overheating and premature failure; specifying S4 unnecessarily increases capital cost. Our engineers match the duty classification to your valve's actual operating philosophy.

How often should flame arrestors be inspected?

PROTEGO recommends flame arrestor inspection every 6 months under normal service conditions, reducing to every 3 months in fouling service (waxy crude tanks, polymerising vapours, dusty environments). Inspection involves removing the element bank, checking for plugging or corrosion, cleaning with a compatible solvent (never mechanical scraping), and verifying the flame gap dimensions with a gap gauge. End-of-line arrestors exposed to weather and insects should be inspected more frequently. We supply PROTEGO inspection kits, replacement element banks, and trained technicians for on-site service at Tanzanian tank farms. A blocked flame arrestor is as dangerous as no arrestor at all.
